LLM Agent Security and Privacy

Current security and privacy issues of LLM Agents

24 Apr 2026 · 5 min read · Research piece

What is an LLM

A large language model (LLM) is a type of artificial intelligence trained on vast amounts of data to interpret and produce human-like language. The field of LLM security and privacy examines potential risks, aiming to detect, evaluate, and mitigate threats associated with their use. Although these models are capable of tasks such as text generation, language translation, and question answering, they also present significant challenges, particularly in terms of security weaknesses and privacy risks.

What is an LLM Agent

An LLM agent is distinct from an LLM as it is able to go beyond simply generating text. It can understand instructions, make decisions, use tools, and take actions in order to complete tasks, often by interacting with users or external systems in a more autonomous way (K2View, 2025)

LLM Agent Risks

Current LLM agents used today are improving in complexity but still maintain traditional machine learning risks, such as data poisoning or adversarial attacks. Due to their ability to interact with users, use external tools, and make decisions, these risks can have more serious consequences compared to earlier models. For example, vulnerabilities may lead to the:

"...leaking [of] sensitive information, spreading misinformation, or even executing harmful actions unintentionally" (He et al., 2025).

In addition to inherited risks, LLM agents also introduce new, agent-specific threats. These can include the manipulation of outputs, any interferences with decision making processes, and corruption of the data they rely on. As these systems become utilised more across many different industries, addressing these concerns is becoming increasingly important to ensure their reliability and trustworthiness (He et al., 2025).

LLM Agent Evaluation

In order to determine the best methods of enforcing LLM agent security, it is important to consider proper evaluation methods. Due to the difficulty involved in evaluating LLM agents which operate in dynamic environments, compared to evaluating normal LLM calls which do not, some proposed methods include using a final response evaluation process (Black-Box) and a trajectory evaluation process (Grey-Box).

Black-Box evaluation only looks at the agent's final output, without any visibility into how it got there. This makes it simple to apply, but it can miss a lot. An agent could arrive at the right answer through a flawed or even dangerous process, and Black-Box evaluation would not catch that (Zhang et al., 2026).

Grey-Box evaluation sits in the middle. Rather than only checking the final output, it looks at the sequence of tools the agent used along the way. This gives a clearer picture of how the agent behaved and helps uncover risky or unexpected actions that a purely outcome-focused approach would miss (Zhang et al., 2026).

Conclusion

Overall, the security and privacy risks associated with LLM agents are still not fully understood and continue to grow as the technology evolves. As these systems become more advanced and widely used, the impact of their vulnerabilities becomes increasingly difficult to overlook. These risks range from inherited issues like data poisoning and adversarial attacks to more agent-specific threats such as prompt injection and output manipulation, showing that the overall attack surface is broad and expanding.

While evaluation approaches like black-box and grey-box testing are useful, current research suggests that no single method is enough on its own to fully address these challenges (Zhang et al., 2026). What is clear is that security and privacy need to be considered from the beginning of development, rather than treated as an afterthought, requiring ongoing effort from researchers, developers, and organisations using these systems.

Key Terms

  • Data poisoning: A type of cyberattack where corrupted or malicious data is introduced into a model's training process, potentially causing it to produce inaccurate or harmful outputs (Krantz and Jonker, 2024).
  • Adversarial attacks: Deliberately crafted inputs designed to manipulate a model’s behaviour or force it to produce incorrect or misleading results (He et al., 2025).
